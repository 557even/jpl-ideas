Title
-----
Complete Understanding of Dark Matter

Abstract
--------
Dark matter is inferred from gravitational phenomena across scales—from galaxies to the cosmic microwave background—
yet remains unidentified at the particle level. This text integrates observational evidence, cosmological inference,
particle-physics candidates, and experimental searches, while critically examining alternatives (modified gravity),
small-scale tensions, and beyond-CDM scenarios. Emphasis is placed on inference pipelines: how data constrain models,
how systematics propagate, and what future measurements can decisively discriminate.

How to Read This Book
---------------------
- If you are observationally oriented: start with Parts II and V, then Part III.
- If you are particle-physics oriented: start with Chapters 7–8, then Chapters 10–13, then Part II.
- If you are methods oriented: read Part VII in parallel with Parts II–IV.

Notation and Conventions
------------------------
- Newton’s constant: G
- Hubble parameter: H(t), H0 at present epoch
- Density parameters: Ω_i = ρ_i / ρ_crit, with ρ_crit = 3H0^2 / (8πG)
- Scale factor: a(t), with a=1 today
- Comoving wavenumber: k
- Velocity dispersion: σ
- DM density profiles: ρ(r)
- Particle DM: mass mχ, annihilation cross section ⟨σv⟩, scattering cross section σχN

CHAPTER 1 — INTRODUCTION TO DARK MATTER
======================================

1.1 What is Dark Matter?
------------------------
“Dark matter” denotes a component of the universe that:
(1) gravitated strongly enough to shape structure and dynamics,
(2) emits/absorbs negligible electromagnetic radiation in observed bands, and
(3) behaves, to leading order on large scales, like pressureless matter.

Operationally, dark matter is an inference: it is whatever extra source term in gravity (within GR or Newtonian limits)
is required to reconcile dynamical or lensing mass with luminous (baryonic) mass.

1.2 The Missing Mass Problem
----------------------------
Many systems exhibit gravitational potentials inconsistent with baryons alone:
- Galaxy rotation curves remain flat at large radii rather than falling as v(r) ~ r^{-1/2}.
- Cluster galaxies move too fast for visible mass (virial discrepancy).
- Gravitational lensing maps show more mass than starlight/gas indicate.
- Cosmological observables (CMB, large-scale structure) require a non-baryonic matter density.

The common thread is the relation between gravitational field and source distribution.

1.3 Dark Matter vs. Dark Energy
-------------------------------
- Dark matter clusters, deepens potentials, and seeds structure.
- Dark energy drives accelerated expansion and is approximately smooth on large scales.

In ΛCDM, dark matter is a matter component (w≈0), dark energy is Λ (w≈−1).

1.4 Importance in Modern Cosmology
----------------------------------
Dark matter is central to:
- Growth of structure: perturbations in DM begin growing earlier than baryonic perturbations.
- Halo formation: galaxies form within DM halos.
- Precision cosmology: Ω_m, Ω_b, and initial spectrum parameters are tightly coupled in CMB fits.

1.5 Scope and Structure
-----------------------
This text proceeds from inference (evidence) to hypothesis space (candidates/models) to falsification (experiments),
while explicitly treating alternatives and known anomalies.

CHAPTER 2 — HISTORICAL DEVELOPMENT
=================================

2.1 Early Observations (1930s–1970s)
------------------------------------
2.1.1 Zwicky and the Coma Cluster (1933)
- Applied virial arguments to cluster galaxies; inferred mass far exceeding luminous matter.
- Introduced the idea of “missing mass” at cluster scale.

2.1.2 Sinclair Smith and Virgo
- Similar conclusions: high velocity dispersions imply deep potentials not matched by visible matter.

2.1.3 Jan Oort’s Galactic Mass Estimates
- Early local dynamical mass estimates in the Milky Way highlighted mass discrepancies and motivated systematic study
  of stellar kinematics and disk mass.

2.2 Vera Rubin and Rotation Curves (1970s)
------------------------------------------
2.2.1 Andromeda Observations
- Measured stellar/gas rotation at large radii.

2.2.2 Flat Rotation Curves
For circular orbits:
  v(r)^2 / r = GM(<r)/r^2  =>  v(r)^2 = GM(<r)/r
If v(r) ≈ constant at large r, then M(<r) ∝ r, implying extended mass distribution.

2.2.3 Implications
- Requires a halo more extended than the luminous disk.
- Shifts the “missing mass” problem from clusters to ordinary galaxies.

2.3 Theoretical Developments
----------------------------
2.3.1 WIMP Hypothesis Formation
- Particle-physics motivated weak-scale relics naturally produce the right cosmic abundance in thermal freeze-out
  (the “WIMP miracle,” treated carefully in Chapter 7/8).

2.3.2 Alternatives (MOND, Modified Gravity)
- Proposed that dynamics change at low acceleration; later embedded in relativistic frameworks.

2.3.3 Evolution of Detection Strategies
- From astronomical inference to three-pronged search program:
  (1) direct detection, (2) indirect detection, (3) collider/production searches.

CHAPTER 3 — FUNDAMENTAL PHYSICS PREREQUISITES
============================================

3.1 Standard Model Overview
---------------------------
3.1.1 Fermions and Bosons
- Matter: quarks/leptons; forces: gauge bosons; scalar: Higgs.

3.1.2 Four Fundamental Forces
- EM, weak, strong, gravity (GR).
- Dark matter must couple gravitationally; additional couplings may be feeble or hidden.

3.1.3 Limitations of the Standard Model
- No viable stable, cold, non-baryonic DM candidate with correct abundance and clustering behavior.
- Neutrinos are too light (hot DM) to form observed small-scale structures alone.

3.2 GR and Cosmology
--------------------
3.2.1 Curvature
- Matter-energy determines curvature; geodesics encode motion/light deflection.

3.2.2 Einstein Field Equations
  G_{μν} + Λ g_{μν} = 8πG T_{μν}

3.2.3 FLRW Metric
Homogeneity/isotropy leads to:
  ds^2 = -dt^2 + a(t)^2 [ dr^2/(1-kr^2) + r^2 dΩ^2 ]

Friedmann equation:
  H^2 = (8πG/3) ρ - k/a^2 + Λ/3

3.3 Statistical Mechanics and the Early Universe
-----------------------------------------------
3.3.1 Boltzmann Distribution and Relics
- Thermal populations depend on exp(-E/T).
- Freeze-out occurs when interaction rate Γ drops below expansion rate H.

3.3.2 Phase Space Density
- Liouville’s theorem constrains coarse-grained phase-space evolution and informs warm/fuzzy DM bounds.

3.3.3 Thermal Equilibrium
- Early universe plasma maintains equilibrium until decoupling.

3.4 QFT Basics
--------------
3.4.1 Feynman Diagrams
- Organize perturbative interactions and compute amplitudes.

3.4.2 Cross Sections
- Experimental searches translate null results into limits on σχN or ⟨σv⟩.

3.4.3 Symmetries
- Stabilizing symmetries (e.g., Z2 parity) often invoked to prevent DM decay.

CHAPTER 4 — GALACTIC-SCALE EVIDENCE

4.1 Rotation Curves
-------------------
4.1.1 Newtonian Expectation
For a centrally concentrated mass:
  v(r) ~ sqrt(GM/r)  (Keplerian decline)

4.1.2 Observation
Many spiral galaxies show v(r) ≈ constant beyond optical radius.

4.1.3 Mass Models
Decompose:
  v^2(r) = v_baryon^2(r) + v_DM^2(r)
Baryons include disk + bulge + gas; DM halo adds extended component.

4.1.4 Halo Profiles
NFW:
  ρ(r) = ρ_s / [(r/r_s)(1+r/r_s)^2]
Einasto:
  ln[ρ(r)/ρ_s] = -(2/α)[(r/r_s)^α - 1]

Interpretation:
- NFW emerges in collisionless CDM simulations.
- Deviations at small radii motivate baryonic feedback or alternative DM physics (SIDM, fuzzy DM).

4.2 Dwarf Spheroidal Galaxies
-----------------------------
4.2.1 Mass-to-Light
Large M/L ratios imply deep potentials with little luminous matter.

4.2.2 Velocity Dispersions
Under equilibrium assumptions, Jeans modeling links σ(r) to ρ(r) but suffers degeneracies with anisotropy β(r).

4.2.3 DM Dominated Systems
Dwarfs are prime laboratories:
- high DM fraction,
- low astrophysical backgrounds for gamma-ray searches,
- sensitive to small-scale physics (free-streaming, cores).

4.3 Ellipticals and Stellar Dynamics
------------------------------------
4.3.1 X-ray Observations
Hot gas in ellipticals traces potential via hydrostatic equilibrium (with systematics from non-thermal pressure).

4.3.2 Gas Distributions
X-ray surface brightness + temperature profiles constrain mass.

4.3.3 Lensing at Galactic Scale
Strong lensing (Einstein rings/arcs) and weak lensing provide mass measurements largely independent of dynamical state.

CHAPTER 5 — CLUSTERS AND LARGE-SCALE STRUCTURE
=============================================

5.1 Cluster Mass Measurements
-----------------------------
5.1.1 Virial Theorem
For a bound system:
  2⟨T⟩ + ⟨U⟩ = 0
Velocity dispersions imply total mass well above luminous mass.

5.1.2 X-ray Temperature/Luminosity
Assuming hydrostatic equilibrium:
  dP/dr = -ρ_g(r) GM(<r)/r^2

5.1.3 Sunyaev–Zel’dovich Effect
Inverse Compton scattering of CMB photons off hot electrons provides pressure-integrated measures complementing X-ray.

5.2 Gravitational Lensing
-------------------------
5.2.1 Strong Lensing
Multiple images/arcs constrain projected mass in inner regions.

5.2.2 Weak Lensing
Statistical shear of background galaxies maps mass over large scales.

5.2.3 Bullet Cluster-Type Evidence
In some merging systems, lensing mass peaks are spatially offset from X-ray gas (dominant baryonic component),
suggesting most gravitating mass is collisionless and does not follow shocked baryons.

5.2.4 Anomalies (e.g., complex mergers)
Some systems show complicated mass-gas alignments; these motivate careful treatment of projection effects,
non-equilibrium dynamics, and selection biases.

5.3 Large-Scale Structure Formation
-----------------------------------
5.3.1 Surveys
Galaxy redshift surveys measure clustering, growth, and BAO.

5.3.2 Cosmic Web
CDM naturally forms filaments, walls, voids from Gaussian initial conditions.

5.3.3 BAO
Sound waves in the early baryon-photon plasma imprint a characteristic scale used as a standard ruler.

### 1.1 What is Dark Matter?

Dark Matter (DM) is a non-baryonic, collisionless, and non-relativistic component of the universe that dominates the gravitational mass of galactic and extragalactic structures. ### 5.2 Gravitational Lensing

Lensing is the deflection of light from distant sources by foreground mass.

### 5.3 The Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-558)

The Bullet Cluster provides the most direct evidence that DM is collisionless. In this merging system, the X-ray gas (the main baryonic component) interacted and slowed down due to ram pressure (shown in pink). ## Chapter 2: Historical Development

### 2.1 Early Observations (1930s-1970s)

#### 2.1.1 Fritz Zwicky and the Coma Cluster (1933)

Swiss physicist Fritz Zwicky, while observing the Coma Cluster at the Mount Wilson Observatory, used the virial theorem to calculate the cluster's mass. He found that the galaxies were moving at velocities far exceeding the escape velocity calculated from the visible stars. He coined the term *dunkle Materie* (dark matter), suggesting that an invisible substance provided the necessary gravity to keep the cluster bound.

#### 2.1.2 Sinclair Smith's Virgo Cluster Studies

In 1936, Sinclair Smith performed a similar analysis on the Virgo Cluster. He found a mass-to-light ratio so high that he hypothesized the existence of "intergalactic matter" or "great mass associated with each nebula" to account for the velocities.

#### 2.1.3 Jan Oort's Galactic Mass Estimates

In 1932, Jan Oort analyzed the vertical kinematics of stars in the Milky Way. Though his specific calculations (the "Oort Limit") were later refined, he was the first to attempt to weigh the local galaxy using the motion of stars perpendicular to the galactic plane, establishing the precedent for dynamical mass measurements.
